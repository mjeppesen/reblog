Zepnip Tufeki on Game of Thrones and storytelling

Matthew Jeppesen

07 May 2021 • 2 min read

Zeynip Tufeki on why the last season of Game of Thrones felt so different — and disappointing to so many fans.
Essentially, she argues that the story changed from one about an entire world, with all kinds of people, and institutions, nations, into one that was  almost entirely about a small group of characters.

One way to see how different early GoT was:

is clearly the show’s willingness to kill off major characters, early and often, without losing the thread of the story.
The appeal of a show that routinely kills major characters signals a different kind of storytelling, where a single charismatic and/or powerful individual, along with his or her internal dynamics, doesn’t carry the whole narrative and explanatory burden.

This kind of storytelling:

encourages us to put ourselves in the place of any character, not just the main hero/heroine, and imagine ourselves making similar choices. “Yeah, I can see myself doing that under such circumstances.”

This is a really interesting article.
The world of Game of Thrones is enormous and feels real, and when you imagine yourself in that world, the weight of that world — meaning the people, the institutions, and so on —  is so apparent.

If you were in that world and you were a simple peasant, and you wanted to protest for better treatment or for "no taxation without representation", or what have you, well, you can just see all the ways that be hard and unlikely to work in such a world.  Other peasants would probably not support you. They would never have seen anything like it before. They would not believe that it could work. They might suspect that you were mad or had some secret plan to steal power. They would fear revenge from the local lord, which is a sensible fear since it probably would happen.

On the other hand, there is a kind of justice in that world. Everyone in the world has a sense of right and wrong — just as that they do here, but they have their place and they have their interests and loyalties and desires, and … well, it's a big and interesting world.

Similarly to this, I remember once hearing John Hodgman say that it was interesting to him that Game of Thrones was so popular, given that it is not set in a world that anyone would want to live in. (Unlike say, that of The Lord of the Rings).

Anyway, the article from Zepney Tufeki (here is the link again) really had me thinking about it for a long time after I read it. I recommend it.
